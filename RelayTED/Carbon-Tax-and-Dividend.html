We Need a Carbon Tax and Dividend.

What it is, and why I believe it is the most efficient path forward.

Written by Sarthak Kamat

At a TEDx conference we organized at Monta Vista High School recently, I met with Tim Dec, an activist from the Citizens’ Climate Lobby who lobbied 90+ congressmen in a bipartisan push for an idea called the Carbon Tax and Dividend. I have been a long-time proponent of this idea -- and this talk in particular inspired me to write this blog.

Climate Change and Wildfires

 As residents of California, we have seen the adverse effects of climate change that facilitate the necessary conditions for devastating forest fires to torment major cities. Increased global warming is largely to blame for creating dry atmospheres and extreme temperatures, that cause small wildfires to magnify into larger ones. In fact, the past century has seen a three degree increase in California temperatures, creating an additional warmth in the air that dries plants, leaving them in prime condition to burn. These conditions lead to frequent forest fires, whose concurrent release of trapped carbon only adds to the incessant positive feedback loop that creates more damaging conditions for the state. In fact, U.S. Interior Secretary Ryan Zinke reported to NBC News that a 2018 California wildfire released nearly 68 million tons of CO2, roughly the same amount emitted by electricity production for the entire state. The associated $9 billion in property damage and the 88 lives unnecessarily lost in these recent California fires provides a stronger reason in decreasing carbon emissions.

Carbon Tax

Even though the most adverse effects of global warming are felt by us, the people, the corporations that are responsible for the emission disproportionately see the benefits. This is mainly because there is no real incentive to reduce CO2 emissions from a purely economic standpoint. In fact, our present economic measurements do not reflect the environmental externalities to monetary optimizations, and instead reward them -- the rebuilding necessitated by hurricanes, wildfires, or lead poisoning in our pipes are all reflected positively in our GDP. There is an effective way to address this problem that would begin to price these externalities, and align economic incentives to environmental ones: a carbon tax.

A carbon tax can easily be instituted by charging companies for every metric ton of carbon dioxide emitted, thus measuring the damage these companies are doing to non-shareholders such as the earth. We can also issue this tax equivalently on imports from countries without an equivalent carbon tax. Without necessitating any governmental intervention, a carbon tax would allow businesses to create market-based solutions to pollution and push for investment in greener forms of energy. Further, A 2017 study from the US Treasury estimates a tax of $49 per metric ton of carbon dioxide nationally could raise about $2.2 trillion over 10 years from 2019 to 2028.

The Dividend

With a dividend in place, we can give every American an equal slice of the revenue generated by a Carbon Tax. This allows us to go beyond pricing in the externalities for polluters, to returning the net economic loss back to the consumers. This tax revenue can also be used in more targeted programs -- such as constructively towards helping those most affected by climate disasters or investing in more renewable technologies. Such a program would be, in effect, a net-zero cost to the US Federal Budget.

Let’s do it.

Similar carbon taxes have already been implemented to great success in a number of countries and regions, and it is time we join them in creating economic incentives for a green economy, right here in the United States.
