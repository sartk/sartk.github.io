Healthcare shouldn’t be tied to employment.

Why the existing system makes us unfree.

Written by Sarthak Kamat

We have a messy healthcare system, at this awkward mid point between single-payer and an entirely private system. Our healthcare is tied to our employment in ways that actually hinder entrepreneurship and creativity.

What’s the situation

Healthcare is a crushing burden on America. We spend twice as much as most developed nations on healthcare, to significantly worse outcomes. As automation renders jobs become less permanent, 96% of newly created jobs are temp gig/contractor jobs with no healthcare benefits. Small businesses struggle with the massive healthcare costs that they have to deal with.

For consumers, healthcare bills are the number one cause of personal bankruptcy and 56 million Americans are struggling with healthcare costs. Half of Americans can’t afford to pay for an unexpected 500 dollar medical procedure.

And now, because people’s healthcare is tied to employment, they can’t afford to leave their jobs to start businesses. They can’t afford to pursue creative endeavors. New parents can’t leave their jobs to take care of their kids. This is not a good thing.

And if you somehow manage to figure out your healthcare and start a successful business, the cost of paying for your employee’s insurance is immeasurably high. This leads to even more automation or contractor jobs, creating a miserable cycle of bad healthcare coverage.


At some point, we have to take this boot off of people’s throats.

The potential

There are two solutions that would be infinitely better than the system we have today -- either implement a single payer system, where the government pays for everyone’s healthcare or a fully private system.

Our current healthcare system does the last thing we want to do. It discourages businesses from hiring employees because of the massive impediment on their costs. If we could reduce every single business’s healthcare costs by $2500 per month per employee, wages would skyrocket and we would create more jobs.

We could remove the “job lock,” allow people to change jobs more easily, and increase dynamism in the labor market. We would decrease sources of stress and actually improve the mental health of millions of Americans, if they knew they didn’t have to worry about being able to afford to be sick.

All of a sudden, you would have insurance companies competing with each other to negotiate down costs. You would have drug companies competing for affordable prices.

Imagine if we could actually look up costs of check-ups at local physicians and go for the cheapest one. Prices would drop dramatically.

In conclusion

In the words of Walter Cronkite, “Our healthcare system is neither healthy, caring, nor a system.” It’s a crapshoot.
