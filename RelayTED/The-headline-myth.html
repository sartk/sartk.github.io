The Headline Myth.

Why we’re looking at the wrong numbers when we talk about GDP and unemployment, and what we should be doing instead.

Written by Sarthak Kamat

Just recently, Nobel Prize winning economist Joseph Stiglitz proposed that it’s time to kill the GDP. And he’s right.

Case in point

Our modern economic system is working exactly as it was intended -- to maximize the profit on every dollar spent. This has been an incredibly successful model that has uplifted millions out of poverty.

As we enter the 21st century, an era with unprecedented technological progress, it will still work as intended. But with a major caveat -- that it will no longer be good for humanity. As Stiglitz argues, GDP gives us “no hint of environmental degradation or resource depletion, nor inequality, middle-class suffering, or lower standards of living.” At some point, we have to recognize that this system is no longer aligned with our general well being.

Our GDP is growing and growing, up to over 20 trillion in the last decade. Our headline unemployment is at an all time low and our economy at all time high.

You’d think everything is great with those numbers. It’s not. What else is at all time high? Suicide. Drug overdose. Depression. In fact, we are the only developed nation to see a decrease in life expectancy for three years in a row. These kinds of numbers are unseen in any modern western economy. Half of the next generation’s children are slated to have a worse future than their parents. This figure was over 90% in the 50s.

And all those jobs we were talking about, 96% of them are temp gig and contractor jobs, with no healthcare benefits or employer assistance.

The robots are coming

This problem will only be made worse with advances in technology and AI. A quarter of American jobs are already at high risk of being automated. Among these: truck drivers, call center workers, fast food workers, retail workers. What are we going to do when these jobs are displaced? If we keep chasing the traditional methods of capital efficiency, we lose big time to the robots and the software.

It’s a race off a cliff. We will see unprecedented levels of unemployment, famine, poverty, self-destructive behavior, that will be unstoppable. All the gains of the automation would be captured on top.

Picture an alternate reality. Where automation doesn’t make us poor and destitute. Instead, where it would free us to do the kinds of work we want to do -- to care for our families, to pursue creative endeavors, to give back to our communities. This should be our vision.

Instead of us working for the economy, the economy should be working for us.  The first step to having that new economy that works for us is to change the measurements.

New Measurements

Stiglitz’s new book Measuring What Counts: The Global Movement for Well-Being, cowritten with French economists Jean-Paul Fitoussi and Martine Durand, demonstrate how we can use better metrics that account for details such as human sustainability and -- yes, really -- “how people feel about their lives”.

The first thing we should do therefore is to now, apart from the GDP, also start measuring things like:

  • Life expectancy
  • Standard of living
  • Mental Health
  • Freedom from substance abuse
  • Quality of air and drinking water
  • Success rates for children
  • Income equality


This is the real vision of an economy that works for us in the 21st century.
