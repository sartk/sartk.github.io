Should we regulate tech companies?

A detailed look at the case for, the case against, and the best path forward. By Sarthak Kamat.

Clearly, mishandling of personal data by tech companies is a very real issue today, and must be addressed. A recent example of this is the highly publicized Facebook-Cambridge Analytica scandal — where “personally identifiable information of ‘up to 87 million people’” was collected “by the political consulting firm Cambridge Analytica” (Confessore). The pressing data privacy issue has created several factions over how to resolve this issue -- and whether we should regulate tech.

What’s happening with our data

If you are an avid user of the internet, particularly social media, tech companies likely already know everything about you: how you look, your political and religious beliefs, your health, how old you or your parents are, who you talk to, what your talking about, what kinds of movies your interested in - oftentimes even more than you’d know about yourself.

And you might be wondering -- what do they do with your data? This has often been simplified to it being tech companies selling your data. But that statement isn’t really true. They use this data and algorithmic inferences from that data to target users with ads specifically designed for users, an optimization that improves their revenue drastically. They act as a middle-agent matching advertisers to customers in an efficient way.

How much is that data worth?

A new study came out that our data is now actually more valuable than oil. For Facebook, the ads run using inferences from our data was massive, generating $6.18 per user for facebook in Q4 of 2018, that amounts to roughly $25 per user per year, all without users having to pay a single cent. Other companies like Amazon and Google are so invested in getting users’ data, that they are willing to pay for it. For instance, Amazon even goes to offer $50 off on smartphones that allow Amazon to track users’ data, and this push for users data can be seen in companies’ aggressive push for the smart-speaker market, a new tap to access users data, with smart speaker sales increasing 48% annually.

The case for regulation

Jeremy Gillula, Tech Policy Director at the Electronic Frontier Foundation, in my personal interview with him says that we’ve gone “way too far into using customer data”, to the point that this abuse has ultimately necessitated government regulation in protecting consumer interests. He argues that we need regulation in tech to draw the line over what is and isn’t ethical, and hold companies really accountable to their actions, citing several limited government regulation models, such as that of the European Union, which have succeeded in guaranteeing consumer-business trust in the internet space.

 In my interview with her, Jen King, a PhD and Director of Consumer Privacy at the Center for Internet and Society at Stanford Law School, also argues that since internet platforms have become commodified resources that are almost impossible to live without, the free market can no longer function as intended, tech companies must ultimately be regulated. She supports her argument by citing various studies she has conducted herself - that strongly suggest that user’s care especially about their privacy and how that data is shared; according to her studies, a majority of users are willing to or have stopped using internet services that have misused their data in the past, showing that these restrictions will also benefit tech companies by increasing trust.

 She intends to share her envisioning of a balanced internet regulation structure - a structure where users are the ones who own their data, where at the very minimum there should be types of restrictions on the kinds of information companies collect and on sharing that data with third party companies without explicit user consent, in order to protect both companies and consumers. In fact, there is evidence to Jen King’s suggestions. An  IBM Research Poll states that “78 percent of U.S. respondents say a company's ability to keep their data private is "extremely important" and only 20 percent "completely trust" organizations they interact with to maintain the privacy of their data” (Rowinski). Not only is this statistic alarming for companies, but it also ratifies that tech companies aren’t doing enough to make people feel safe. If consumers don’t feel safe, businesses will eventually plummet because of that lack of trust with the customer.

Does more regulation help businesses?

The Barack Obama administration, in its article “Consumer Data Privacy in a Networked World: A Framework for Protecting Privacy and Promoting Innovation in the Global Digital Economy,” (2012) thus makes a concurrent argument that regulation is better for business. It states that the growth in internet technologies have created the need for stronger privacy protections on the internet, and that those protections will not only benefit consumers, but also tech companies, because it will maintain user trust and allow for the sustenance of the internet-ad business.

Stricter regulation in general hurts small businesses

The truth is, however, that the legal expenses of regulations fall disproportionately on small businesses. According to a US Chamber of Commerce study, the costs to smaller businesses are nearly 20% higher than the average for all firms” (“The Regulatory Impact on Small Business”). By allowing companies to grow without governmental regulation, we allowed smaller companies to bubble to the top. And when we didn’t, it cost small businesses, and eventually consumers heavily. The same study states that Federal regulations alone are estimated to cost the American economy as much as $1.9 trillion a year in direct costs, lost productivity, and higher prices.

Legal costs fall disproportionately on smaller firms.When small businesses fail, the free market fails too, and consumers lose control.

Pushing power to unelected bureaucrats

A second argument against regulation is that if the government were to regulate, it would actually move power away from the consumer and toward unelected bureaucrats and regulators in commissions like the FCC, the FTC, or the EPA. These bureaucrats do not represent the interests of the consumer because they are legally immune from lawsuits and unelected by the American people.

For example in June 2018, FCC voted to repeal neutrality even as 83 percent of people nationwide supported KEEPING these net neutrality rules in according to an independent poll from the University of Maryland’s Program for Public Consultation in December 2017 (Neidig). Although the merits of that move is debatable, it is clear that at a time when we cannot trust these regulatory organizations to stand for what the consumers believe in, moving even more power to unelected and unaccountable bureaucrats would be grossly damaging.

And so … where do we go from here?

Many pro-regulation advocates continue to argue that when we give companies too much liberty, they are incentivized to exploit customers to get ahead of their competition rather than compete ethically, and that therefore, some amount of regulation is necessary to safeguard consumer interests from potentially deleterious consequences.

However these regulatory demands have to be viewed within the light of the side effects of governmental regulation. The tech self-regulation model, instead, addresses both the consumer protection concerns and these side effects, and is an ideal solution to this problem.

Under the self-regulation model, companies make mutually beneficial agreements over how they will collect and use consumer data. The Digital Advertising Alliance is a successful example. It is an alliance of all the major internet services, from Facebook to Google to Microsoft, that offers consumers a one-click option to opt-out of all activity tracking on the internet, and “establishes and enforces responsible privacy practices across the industry” (“About the Digital Advertising Alliance”).

In June 2016, the Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada released a research study to see if advertisers adhered to the Digital Advertising Alliance opt-out request. The OPC study found that advertisers adhered to opt-out requests 98% of the time (“Online Behavioral Advertising Follow Up Research Project”). Meaning that the DAA’s offering not only successfully protects consumer privacy, it draws a pathway for self-regulation to be undertaken in the future.

Self-regulation is, ultimately, the smartest step we can take to meet the needs of the American consumer, without disrupting the free market economics that made the industry successful in the first place. By keeping the government out of regulation, we restore consumer control through the free market, protect small businesses from legal hindrances, and maintain companies’ liberties, all at once.
